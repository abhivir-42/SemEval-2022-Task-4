\begin{abstract}
We address SemEval 2022 Task 4 Subtask 1: binary classification of paragraphs as containing Patronising and Condescending Language (PCL) towards vulnerable communities. The dataset exhibits a severe class imbalance ($\sim$9.5:1 negative to positive ratio), which the provided RoBERTa-base baseline handles through downsampling, achieving F1\,=\,0.48 on the official dev set. We propose a context-enriched DeBERTa-v3-base approach with three novel components: (1) community-aware input representation that prepends the target community keyword to each paragraph, (2) focal loss to handle class imbalance without discarding training data, and (3) systematic threshold optimisation to maximise F1. Our approach achieves F1\,=\,0.59 on the official dev set, a 22.8\% relative improvement over the baseline. We present a detailed error analysis examining model failures across community keywords and PCL categories.

\end{abstract}

\chapter{Critical Paper Review}

We review ``Don't Patronize Me! An Annotated Dataset with Patronizing and Condescending Language towards Vulnerable Communities'' by P\'{e}rez-Almendros, Espinosa-Anke, and Schockaert (COLING 2020) \cite{perezalmendros2020}.

\section{Primary Contributions}

This paper makes three primary contributions. First, it introduces the \textbf{Don't Patronize Me! dataset}, a novel resource of 10,637 paragraphs extracted from news stories across 20 English-speaking countries, annotated for patronising and condescending language directed at vulnerable communities. Second, the authors propose a \textbf{two-level taxonomy of seven PCL categories} organised under three higher-level groups: \textit{The Saviour} (unbalanced power relations, shallow solution), \textit{The Expert} (presupposition, authority voice), and \textit{The Poet} (compassion, metaphor, the poorer the merrier). This taxonomy is grounded in sociolinguistic literature on how media discourse can routinise discrimination. Third, the paper provides \textbf{baseline experiments} across binary PCL detection (Task 1) and multi-label PCL categorisation (Task 2) using SVM, BiLSTM, and transformer-based models, establishing that the task is feasible yet challenging, with RoBERTa achieving the best F1 of 70.68 on Task 1.

\section{Technical Strengths}

\textbf{Novelty and significance of the task framing.} The paper identifies a genuinely underexplored area in harmful language detection: language that is patronising rather than overtly hostile. As the authors argue, PCL differs from hate speech because it is ``generally used unconsciously and with good intentions,'' making it both socially important and technically challenging. At the time of writing, only Wang and Potts (2019) had addressed condescension computationally, and that work focused on direct interpersonal communication rather than media discourse.

\textbf{Carefully designed annotation scheme.} The two-step annotation process is methodologically sound. The use of a 3-point scale (0/1/2) per annotator to handle borderline cases, combined into a 5-point aggregated scale, is a pragmatic approach to managing the inherent subjectivity of the task. The involvement of a third annotator to resolve total disagreements (0 vs.\ 2 cases) adds rigour. Inter-annotator agreement is reported honestly: 41\% Kappa across all labels, rising to 61\% when borderline cases are excluded.

\textbf{Thorough error analysis.} Tables~5 and 6 in the paper provide concrete examples of model failures, which is more informative than aggregate metrics alone. The analysis correctly identifies that categories requiring world knowledge (e.g., Metaphor with F1 of 43.4) are harder to detect, offering actionable directions for future work.

\section{Key Weaknesses}

\textbf{Severe class imbalance is insufficiently addressed.} Only 995 of 10,637 paragraphs ($\sim$9.4\%) are positive examples for Task 1, yet the authors do not discuss how they handled this imbalance during training. There is no mention of oversampling, class weighting, or stratified splitting strategies. The reported metrics would be more convincing with confidence intervals across the 10 folds, as single-point estimates make it difficult to assess statistical significance.

\textbf{No ablation or feature analysis.} The paper benchmarks several models but does not investigate what linguistic features drive predictions. The authors note that words like ``us,'' ``they,'' and ``help'' are strong indicators for Unbalanced Power Relations, but this claim is not supported by any feature importance analysis or attention visualisation. An ablation study (e.g., removing keyword features or testing on out-of-domain vulnerable groups) would strengthen the claims about task difficulty.

\textbf{Span-level annotations are collected but not evaluated.} The annotation process includes span-level annotations using BRAT, yet the experiments treat Task 2 as paragraph-level multi-label classification, discarding span boundary information entirely. Span-level evaluation would be more useful for explaining model predictions.

\textbf{Limited discussion of annotator bias.} The three annotators share backgrounds in ``communication, media and data science,'' but the paper does not discuss how their cultural or demographic backgrounds might influence PCL judgements. Furthermore, the keyword-based paragraph selection strategy introduces a systematic bias: paragraphs mentioning vulnerable communities through indirect references would be missed entirely.

\caption{Overall binary class distribution showing the severe 9.5:1 imbalance between No PCL (9,476 paragraphs, 90.5\%) and PCL (993 paragraphs, 9.5\%).}
\label{fig:class_dist}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{./figures/community_pcl_rates.png}
\caption{PCL prevalence by community keyword. Rates range from 2.8\% (immigrant) to 16.5\% (homeless), indicating substantial variation in how different communities are patronised in media.}
\label{fig:community_rates}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:class_dist} reveals a severe class imbalance of approximately 9.5:1 (No PCL to PCL). Only 993 of the 10,637 paragraphs are labelled as containing patronising language. A naive classifier predicting ``No PCL'' for every input would achieve 90.5\% accuracy, but 0\% F1 on the positive class.

Figure~\ref{fig:community_rates} shows that PCL prevalence varies substantially by community keyword. Communities relating to economic vulnerability, specifically \textit{homeless} (16.5\%), \textit{poor-families} (16.5\%), and \textit{in-need} (16.3\%), have PCL rates approximately 5--6$\times$ higher than \textit{immigrant} (2.8\%) and \textit{migrant} (3.3\%). This suggests that media coverage of economic vulnerability is more prone to patronising framing than coverage of immigration.

\subsection{Impact on Approach}

The 9.5:1 imbalance rules out standard cross-entropy training. The baseline addresses this by downsampling negatives to a 2:1 ratio, discarding $\sim$85\% of negative examples. We instead adopt focal loss \cite{lin2017focal}, which dynamically down-weights easy examples during training, preserving all data while focusing learning on boundary cases (detailed in Section~3.1.2).

The 6$\times$ variation in PCL rates across communities motivates an explicit community-aware input representation, where we prepend the target community keyword to each paragraph (detailed in Section~3.1.1).

Figure~\ref{fig:text_length} and Table~\ref{tab:text_stats} reveal that PCL paragraphs are systematically longer than non-PCL paragraphs. The median word count for PCL texts is 47 words versus 42 for non-PCL, with PCL texts also exhibiting a heavier right tail (95th percentile: 113 vs 100 words).

This difference is intuitive: patronising language often involves elaborate justifications, unsolicited advice, or excessive framing that inflates paragraph length. However, the distributions overlap substantially, so length alone is not a reliable classifier.

\subsection{Impact on Approach}

The overall 95th percentile word count is 102 words. Applying a sub-word expansion factor of $\sim$1.3$\times$ (typical for DeBERTa tokenisers on English text) gives an estimated 95th percentile of $\sim$133 tokens. We set \texttt{max\_sequence\_length\,=\,256}, which captures 99\%+ of paragraphs without excessive padding, balancing information retention against computational efficiency.

\chapter{Proposed Approach}

\section{Approach Description}

We propose \textbf{Context-Enriched DeBERTa with Focal Loss} for PCL detection, building on three novel components beyond the RoBERTa-base baseline.

\subsection{Model Backbone: DeBERTa-v3-base}

We use \texttt{microsoft/deberta-v3-base} \cite{he2021debertav3} as our backbone, replacing the baseline's \texttt{roberta-base} \cite{liu2019roberta}. DeBERTa-v3 offers two architectural advantages: (1) \textbf{disentangled attention} separates content and position information, enabling the model to better capture positional patterns of PCL (e.g., framing language at paragraph openings), and (2) \textbf{ELECTRA-style pre-training} (replaced token detection) provides a more sample-efficient training signal, which is critical when the positive class contains fewer than 800 training examples.

\subsection{Component 1: Community-Aware Input Representation}

Rather than feeding raw paragraph text to the model, we prepend a community context prefix:

\begin{center}
\texttt{[Community: \{keyword\}] \{paragraph text\}}
\end{center}

For example: \texttt{[Community: homeless] After Vatican controversy, McDonald's helps feed homeless in Rome.}

As shown in our EDA (Section~2.1), PCL rates vary 6$\times$ across communities. The prefix enables DeBERTa's attention mechanism to learn community-specific PCL patterns. For instance, ``shallow solution'' PCL clusters with \textit{homeless} and \textit{in-need}, while ``presupposition'' clusters with \textit{women} and \textit{hopeless}.

\subsection{Component 2: Focal Loss}

We replace the baseline's downsampling + cross-entropy with focal loss \cite{lin2017focal}:

\begin{equation}
\text{FL}(p_t) = -\alpha_t (1 - p_t)^\gamma \log(p_t)
\end{equation}

where $p_t$ is the model's estimated probability of the correct class, $\alpha = 0.75$ up-weights the positive class, and $\gamma = 2.0$ modulates the focusing effect. When $p_t$ is high (easy example), $(1 - p_t)^\gamma$ is small, reducing its contribution to the gradient. This focuses training on hard examples near the decision boundary, which is precisely where subtle PCL instances lie.

Compared to the baseline's 2:1 downsampling (which discards $\sim$6,000 negative examples), focal loss trains on \textbf{all 8,375 examples}, preserving signal about what non-patronising language looks like.

\subsection{Component 3: Threshold Optimisation}

With severe class imbalance, the optimal classification threshold is typically not 0.5. After training, we compute the model's predicted probabilities on the official dev set and sweep thresholds from 0.1 to 0.9, selecting the threshold that maximises F1 of the positive class.

\section{Rationale and Expected Outcome}

Our approach addresses three core limitations of the baseline:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Undertrained model} $\rightarrow$ 5 epochs with early stopping (vs.\ baseline's 1 epoch)
\item \textbf{Data wastage from downsampling} $\rightarrow$ focal loss on full dataset
\item \textbf{Suboptimal threshold} $\rightarrow$ systematic F1-maximising threshold search
\end{enumerate}

Additionally, DeBERTa-v3-base is a strictly stronger backbone than RoBERTa-base on NLU benchmarks, and community-aware input provides valuable contextual signal. Based on the SemEval 2022 competition results \cite{semeval2022task4}, where DeBERTa-based systems with focal loss achieved F1 $\sim$0.55--0.65 on the test set, we expect our approach to comfortably exceed the baseline.

\section{Global Evaluation}

Table~\ref{tab:results} presents results on the official dev set. Our model achieves F1\,=\,0.5895, a \textbf{22.8\% relative improvement} over the RoBERTa-base baseline (F1\,=\,0.48). The optimal classification threshold is 0.38, well below the default 0.5, confirming that threshold optimisation is essential under severe class imbalance.

In practice, higher recall is preferable for PCL detection: missing patronising language (false negative) is worse than over-flagging borderline cases (false positive), as the latter can be filtered by human review.

\section{Error Analysis}

The model produces 124 false positives and 64 false negatives on the dev set.

\subsection{False Positives}

The most confident false positive ($p = 0.943$) is a paragraph about religious charity tagged with the \textit{in-need} keyword, discussing the Sermon on the Mount and charitable giving. The model likely triggers on the co-occurrence of religious/charitable framing with a vulnerable community keyword, a pattern that frequently accompanies genuine PCL in the training data but here describes factual reporting. More broadly, false positives cluster around paragraphs that \textit{describe} aid programmes without actually being patronising; the model struggles to distinguish reporting \textit{about} help from patronising \textit{framing} of help.

\subsection{False Negatives}

The most confident false negative ($p = 0.043$) involves a paragraph about disability internship programmes, with an original label of 3 (strong PCL). The model assigns very low PCL probability, suggesting it fails to recognise the implicit presupposition that disabled people need special ``opportunities'' framed as generosity. False negatives tend to involve subtle PCL where the patronising element relies on world knowledge or implicit assumptions rather than explicit lexical markers.

\subsection{Confusion Matrix}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{./figures/confusion_matrix.png}
\caption{Confusion matrix on the dev set at threshold 0.38. The model correctly identifies 135 of 199 PCL instances (68\% recall) while misclassifying 124 non-PCL paragraphs as PCL.}

The model achieves 93.5\% specificity on non-PCL paragraphs but only 67.8\% recall on PCL paragraphs (Figure~\ref{fig:confusion}). This asymmetry reflects the inherent difficulty of detecting subtle PCL compared to recognising clearly non-patronising text.
